Make the Invisible Visible

A typical desktop PC consumes between 80W and 400W depending on your daily usage. Left unchecked over a year of typical daily use, that amounts to 200 to 1000 kWh. In Europe, that translates to roughly €30 to €180 added to your electricity bill, and it can be much more in regions with higher energy costs.

Beyond the financial cost, the average PC produces 100 to 400 kg of CO₂ equivalent per year from electricity consumption alone, varying by your local grid's carbon intensity. Environmentally, that is comparable to driving a gas-powered car for 500 to 2000 kilometers.

Most people have absolutely no idea which applications are responsible for this draw. A modern 3D game renderer and a background browser tab mining crypto or running heavy scripts can both silently drain immense power, differing by upwards of 100W without you noticing. WattSeal makes this hidden consumption completely visible.

With WattSeal, you can effortlessly identify wasteful background processes, understand your real electricity cost on a per-application basis, and make informed decisions to reduce your footprint — all with zero technical knowledge required.

Why We Built WattSeal

Standard operating system tools simply do not tell the whole story. The Windows Task Manager shows CPU percentage, but percentage does not equal watts. A process using 10% of your CPU might be drawing significantly more power than another process using 20%, depending on the specific instructions being executed.

Hardware monitoring utilities like HWiNFO excel at reading precise hardware sensors, but they do not map those sensor readings to the specific software running on your machine. Specialized tools like GPU-Z are limited entirely to the graphics card, while MSI Afterburner provides overlays but doesn't tell you what each app actually costs you in electricity over time. WattSeal is the first application to bridge all of this disparate telemetry into one unified view.

Crucially, a monitoring tool that itself uses significant power would be completely self-defeating. That is why WattSeal is built from the ground up in Rust. It is engineered specifically to consume as little system resources as possible, providing near-zero overhead while running silently in the background.

How It Actually Works

Under the hood, WattSeal relies on a highly optimized, lightweight background process. This daemon continuously reads hardware telemetry directly from the source using standardized interfaces: RAPL (Running Average Power Limit) for Intel and AMD CPUs, NVML for NVIDIA graphics cards, and equivalent sysfs/driver sources for AMD GPUs.

To accurately attribute this system-wide power consumption to individual applications, WattSeal utilizes kernel-level process accounting. It tracks the exact hardware utilization of every active process thread and maps it proportionally against the global power draw. This correlated data is then surfaced to the user interface via fast, low-overhead Inter-Process Communication (IPC).

Privacy and performance are foundational. All historical data is stored completely locally in a standardized SQLite database. Your telemetry never leaves your machine. There are no cloud accounts required, absolutely no tracking pixels, and zero external analytics. WattSeal is entirely open source, allowing any developer to independently verify these claims.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much electricity does an average PC use per year?

A typical desktop PC consumes between 80W and 400W depending on usage. Over a year of daily use, that equals roughly 200 to 1000 kWh, which costs between €30 and €180 in electricity in Europe.

How does WattSeal measure power consumption per application?

WattSeal runs a lightweight background process that reads hardware telemetry directly via RAPL and NVML interfaces. It then uses kernel-level process accounting to accurately attribute the measured power draw to individual running applications in real time.

Can WattSeal show me my carbon footprint from using my PC?

Yes. WattSeal includes a native carbon footprint tracker that automatically converts your physical power consumption into CO₂ equivalents based on the carbon intensity of your local electricity grid.

Does WattSeal work with AMD CPUs and NVIDIA GPUs at the same time?

Yes, WattSeal is entirely cross-vendor compatible. It natively supports hardware from AMD, Intel, and NVIDIA simultaneously, reading the appropriate sensor data regardless of your system configuration.

Will WattSeal itself use significant power or slow my PC down?

No. WattSeal is engineered in Rust specifically to be extremely lightweight. It operates with near-zero overhead, ensuring that the monitoring tool itself does not contribute to your system's power consumption or degrade performance.

How is WattSeal different from HWiNFO or MSI Afterburner?

Unlike HWiNFO or MSI Afterburner which focus primarily on hardware sensor readouts or overlays, WattSeal links hardware power draw directly to individual software processes. It shows you exactly which app is costing you money, rather than just showing total system load.

Is WattSeal free and open source?

Yes, WattSeal is 100% free and open-source software built by the community. You can review the entire source code on GitHub, and your local telemetry data never leaves your machine.

Can I export my power consumption history?

Yes, WattSeal allows you to easily export your complete consumption history. Advanced users can export data as CSV or JSON, or directly access the local SQLite database for completely custom analytics.

Does WattSeal work on laptops as well as desktops?

Yes, WattSeal works seamlessly on both laptops and desktop PCs. On laptops, it can be particularly useful for identifying battery-draining applications that are secretly consuming power in the background.
